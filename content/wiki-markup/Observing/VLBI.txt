Very Long Baseline Interferometry (VLBI) is a form of radio interferometry — just like the SKA — but with one key difference: instead of many antennas located within a single site or region, VLBI combines radio telescopes separated by hundreds to thousands of kilometres. These telescopes may be spread across a country, across continents, or even placed in space (so-called Space-VLBI). By linking widely separated antennas, VLBI achieves extremely high angular resolution — far finer than any single radio telescope could reach on its own.

In VLBI observations, the SKA telescopes participate by forming one or more tied-array (phased) beams, which act as elements within a larger global VLBI network.

This means that:

* SKA does not replace existing VLBI networks — it joins them as a highly versatile contributor.
* When the core of an SKA telescope is phased up, it can act as an exceptionally large and sensitive single-dish radio telescope within the global array.
* The SKA can also form multiple independent beams, each of which can participate in VLBI. This enables flexible observing strategies, such as observing a target and calibrator simultaneously, tracking multiple sources, or covering a wider area of sky.
* The geographic location of the SKA telescopes in the southern hemisphere strengthens global VLBI coverage, particularly for southern sky targets, complementing existing facilities in Australia and South Africa while helping balance the historically denser northern-hemisphere distribution of VLBI antennas.
